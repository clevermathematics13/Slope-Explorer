📐 What Are We Building?

An introduction to approximating curves step by step

The Mystery Curve

Imagine there's a curve hiding in the coordinate plane. You can't see it — but you have two clues:

Clue 1 — A Starting Point

The curve passes through the point (0, 1). We write this as:

f(0) = 1

This is called the initial condition — it tells us where the curve begins.

Clue 2 — A Slope Rule

At every point on the curve, the slope equals the y-value. We write this as:

Δy/Δx = y

This is called a differential equation — a rule that connects the slope of a curve to its position.

Together, these two clues completely determine the curve. Our job? Approximate it using only arithmetic — no calculus needed.

Quick Review: What Is Slope?

Slope measures how steep a line is. It's the ratio of how much you go up (rise) to how far you go across (run).

slope = Δy / Δx = rise / run

Example

If you move 1 unit to the right (Δx = 1) and go up 3 units (Δy = 3), the slope is 3.

A steeper line has a bigger slope. A flat line has slope 0.

The Big Idea: Walking the Curve

Here's the key insight. Even though we can't see the whole curve, we know:

  1. Where we start — the point (0, 1)
  2. What direction to walk — the slope rule tells us

So we can take small straight-line steps in the direction of the slope, like a hiker following a compass in the fog:

🥾 Imagine this: You're standing on a hillside in thick fog. You can't see the trail ahead, but your compass (the slope rule) tells you which direction is uphill from where you're standing right now. So you take one step in that direction, check the compass again, and repeat. Each step is straight, but the full walk approximates the winding trail.

This process is called Euler's Method (named after the mathematician Leonhard Euler). Each straight-line segment follows the slope at the current point.

The Step-by-Step Process

Here's what we'll do in the Path Builder Challenge:

Step 1 — Check the slope

Use the slope rule to find the slope at the current point. Our rule says: slope = y-value.

Step 2 — Walk forward

Move a fixed distance to the right (that distance is h, the step size). Use the slope to calculate how far up you go:

ynew = yold + slope × h

Step 3 — Repeat

You're now at a new point. Check the slope again and take another step. Each step adds one segment to your path.

Key Vocabulary

  • Differential equation — A rule that relates the slope of a curve to its coordinates (e.g., Δy/Δx = y)
  • Initial condition — The known starting point of the curve (e.g., f(0) = 1)
  • Step size (h) — The horizontal distance of each step. Smaller h = more accurate path
  • Euler's Method — Approximating a curve by taking small straight-line steps using the slope rule
  • Slope (m) — How steep the curve is at a point. In our problem, slope = y-value
  • Approximation — An estimate that gets closer to the real answer with more/smaller steps
