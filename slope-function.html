⚙️ The Slope Machine

Understanding a function that outputs slope instead of height

A New Kind of Function

Most functions you've seen take an x-value and give back a y-value:

f(x) = 2x + 3 → input x, output a height

But our differential equation is different. It's a rule that takes a point and gives back a slope:

Δy/Δx = y → input a point, output a slope

This might feel weird at first — and that's okay! Let's build intuition with a machine metaphor.

Meet the Slope Machine

Think of the slope rule as a machine. You feed it a point (a y-value), and it tells you the slope at that location.

You feed in
y-value
→
The machine (rule)
slope = y
→
It outputs
slope

Our particular machine has a very simple rule: the slope equals the y-value. That's it!

What does this mean in plain English?

When the curve is high up (big y), it's steep (big slope). When the curve is low (small y), it's gentle (small slope). The higher you are, the faster you climb.

🔍 What Did You Notice?

The Pattern

Every time, the slope is exactly the y-value. It doesn't matter what x is — only the height matters.

At y = 1, the slope is gentle (1). At y = 10, it's very steep (10). At y = 0.5, it's barely rising (0.5).

🏔️ Think of it this way: Imagine a hill where the steepness depends on your altitude. At sea level, the ground is nearly flat. But the higher you climb, the steeper it gets —– like a mountain that curves up sharply the higher you go. That's exactly what Δy/Δx = y describes.

Why This Matters

In the Path Builder Challenge, you'll use this slope machine at every step:

The Euler Method Loop

1. Look at your current point → read its y-value

2. Feed y into the slope machine → get the slope

3. Use the slope to take a step → arrive at a new point

4. Repeat from step 1

The slope machine is the engine that drives every step. Now that you understand how it works, you're ready to build paths!
